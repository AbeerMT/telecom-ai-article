Google just priced Gemini Flash-Lite at $0.25 per million tokens. That single number should trigger a complete rewrite of every telecom AI investment thesis written before 2025.

Not because it is cheap. Because of what cheap inference has always done to industries that did not respond to it correctly — and because telecom has already lived through an almost identical moment, made the wrong moves, and paid for it over a decade.

That moment was 2006. The technology was cloud compute. The price signal was AWS EC2 at $0.10 per hour. The pattern that followed is the most important strategic template available to any telecom leader thinking about AI today.

Part One

The Cloud Story Telcos Remember — and the One They Should

The popular version of the cloud story goes like this: companies moved their infrastructure to AWS, reduced costs, and got faster. True, but incomplete — and the missing part is what matters.

The accurate version: companies that treated cloud as a cost optimisation tool extracted a one-time efficiency gain and built nothing durable. Companies that asked "what does near-zero compute cost make possible that was previously impossible?" built the most valuable businesses of the last twenty years on the answer to that question.

2006
AWS launches EC2 at $0.10/hr
Incumbents evaluate it on existing workloads and find it too unreliable. The right question — what new things does cheap compute enable? — goes unasked.
2008
The cost argument wins internally
Enterprises begin migrating for savings. This is the trap: treating a platform shift as a procurement optimisation. Efficiency gains are real but bounded. Platform advantages are not.
2010
Netflix, Airbnb, Uber build on AWS from zero
Cheap compute did not just reduce costs — it made entirely new business models viable. Companies that could not have afforded on-premise infrastructure at scale now could. Telcos watched this happen to adjacent markets and largely missed the implication.
2013
Telcos build private clouds — the wrong move
Major operators invest heavily in OpenStack deployments to "maintain control." Result: billions in CapEx replicating capabilities AWS delivered better and cheaper. The moat they were trying to build was in entirely the wrong layer.
2015
Twilio: the case that should haunt every telecom strategist
A company with no network assets built a programmable communications layer on commodity cloud and sold it back to the telcos whose infrastructure it was abstracting. The incumbents had better networks. Twilio had the developer relationships and the API standards.
2020
Belated migration — at full hyperscaler price
Most telcos finally move to public cloud at scale — as followers, negotiating from weakness. The window to shape the transition had closed. The catch-up cost is measured in billions and a decade of competitive disadvantage.
2025
AI inference hits the same inflection — right now
Gemini Flash-Lite at $0.25/M. Claude Opus 4.7 with persistent memory. GPT-5.4 with 1M token context. The script is identical. The window is open. The transition will take 3–5 years, not 10. Every pattern is playing out simultaneously and faster.
The companies that won the cloud transition did not just migrate — they rebuilt what they offered. The ones that lost optimised what they already had.

Part Three

The Five Patterns — Playing Out Right Now in AI

History does not repeat, but these five patterns from cloud commoditisation are already visible in how the AI inference story is unfolding. Each one is a decision point — and most telecoms are currently on the wrong side of it.

1
The efficiency trap — treating a platform shift as a cost tool
Most telecom AI programmes today are cost reduction initiatives wearing transformation language. Reducing call centre headcount with AI is real savings — but it is bounded. The companies that will look back on this moment as their defining strategic shift are those asking: what does near-zero inference cost make possible that we could never justify before? AI on every customer interaction. AI-reviewed every network event. AI-personalised every billing statement. These were unjustifiable at $15/M tokens. At $0.25, they are table stakes.
2
The private cloud mistake — investing in the wrong layer
The equivalent today is building a proprietary AI foundation model to avoid hyperscaler dependency. The rationale — control, security, avoiding lock-in — is not wrong. The execution is. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google will deliver better foundation model capabilities than any internal build, faster, and at commodity prices. The investment that creates durable advantage is not in the model layer. It is in the proprietary data infrastructure and fine-tuning capability built on top of commodity models. Redirect the budget.
3
The Twilio threat — someone is building the layer above
Right now, Dialpad, RingCentral, Landbase, and a dozen companies you have not heard of yet are building AI workflow layers — agentic customer journey platforms, network intelligence APIs, autonomous operations stacks — on top of $0.25 commodity inference. They are positioning to sell these back to telcos, exactly as Twilio sold programmable communications back to the operators whose infrastructure it abstracted. The window to build this layer from the inside is measured in months, not years.
4
The digital-native challenger — built on cheap AI from day one
Jio launched in India with a cloud-native stack and a cost structure incumbents could not match without destroying their own margins. Within five years it had 400M subscribers. The AI-native telecom challenger — lighter workforce, fully automated customer operations, AI-driven network management, no legacy BSS/OSS debt — is not a future threat. China Mobile is already running DeepSeek models at 5G edge nodes. The window to build equivalent capability from inside an incumbent is open. It will not stay open.
5
The capability gap — organisational, not technological
Netflix did not win because it had better cloud infrastructure. It won because it built the organisational capability to iterate on software faster than any competitor. The AI equivalent: the telcos that win will build internal AI literacy, data engineering capability, and product iteration speed. You cannot outsource your way to an AI advantage. The companies that outsourced cloud capability to systems integrators in 2012 are still paying for that decision in architecture debt and vendor leverage. Do not repeat it with AI.

Part Five

The Five Strategic Moves to Make in the Next 90 Days

The cloud parallel gives us the playbook. The moves are not complicated — but they require leadership decisions, not technology decisions.

One: Audit every AI vendor contract signed in the last 24 months. A significant portion will be priced against a cost baseline that no longer exists. At $0.25/M tokens, many of those contracts are 10–50× overpriced for the actual computation involved. This is renegotiation leverage you are not currently using.

Two: Reopen every rejected AI use case. Every use case your team killed in the last two years because "the AI cost doesn't justify it" should be reviewed. Running AI on every single customer interaction was unjustifiable at $15/M tokens. At $0.25, it costs under a cent per typical interaction. The assumption that killed those business cases has collapsed.

Three: Shift the budget ratio. Previously: 60% model licensing, 25% integration, 15% data infrastructure. Target state: 20% model licensing, 30% integration depth, 50% proprietary data and fine-tuning. The model spend floor keeps dropping. The data and integration investment compounds. Shift budget toward the compounding asset.

Four: Map your Twilio threat. Identify the companies currently building the AI workflow layer that will compete with or replace your enterprise services. Understand their playbook. Decide whether to partner, acquire, or compete directly — before they have the customer relationships and switching costs that make the decision irreversible.

Five: Start the AI-native operating model conversation at board level. Not as a technology project. As a business model question: what does this company need to look like in five years, and are we designing toward that, or optimising the current model while the window closes?

The window to define the terms of the AI transition is the 18 months we are currently in. After that, you negotiate from weakness — exactly as telcos did when they finally migrated to cloud a decade too late.

The telecom operators who treat commodity AI as a feature to deploy will capture some efficiency gains. The ones who treat it as the same structural platform shift they already lived through with cloud — and this time apply the right strategic response — will build something their competitors cannot replicate in the next funding cycle.

The script is known. The patterns are visible. The question is whether telecom leadership reads the history correctly this time.
